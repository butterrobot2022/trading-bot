Market Dictionary

  • Alpha:

    A measure of an investment's performance compared to a benchmark index, indicating the excess return generated by the investment manager's skills.

  • Bear Market:

    A market characterized by falling prices and pessimism among investors. It is a period when the overall market trend is downward, and investors expect further declines.

  • Bid-Ask Spread:

    The difference between the highest price a buyer is willing to pay (bid) and the lowest price a seller is willing to accept (ask) for a particular security.

  • Blue Chip Stocks:

    Shares of large, well-established, and financially stable companies with a history of reliable performance.

  • Bull Market:

    A market characterized by rising prices and investor optimism. It is a period when the overall market trend is upward, and investors have confidence in the future.

  • Commodity:

    Raw materials or primary agricultural products that are traded on exchanges, such as gold, oil, wheat, etc.

  • Day Trading:

    The practice of buying and selling financial instruments within the same trading day, aiming to profit from short-term price movements.

  • Derivatives:

    Financial contracts or instruments whose value is derived from an underlying asset, index, or reference rate.

  • Dividend:

    A portion of a company's earnings that is distributed to its shareholders on a per-share basis.

  • ETF (Exchange-Traded Fund):

    A type of investment fund that is traded on stock exchanges, representing a diversified portfolio of assets, such as stocks, bonds, or commodities.

  • Earnings Per Share (EPS):

    A company's net earnings divided by the number of outstanding shares, used to assess a company's profitability on a per-share basis.

  • Futures Contract:

    An agreement to buy or sell a specific quantity of an asset at a predetermined price on a future date.

  • IPO (Initial Public Offering):

    The first sale of a company's shares to the public, allowing the company to raise capital from outside investors.

  • Limit Order:

    An order to buy or sell a security at a specific price or better.

  • Long Position:

    Owning a financial asset, such as a stock or commodity, with the expectation that its value will increase over time.

  • Margin:

    The borrowed funds or collateral that an investor uses to trade securities, allowing them to increase their purchasing power.

  • Market Cap (Market Capitalization):

    The total value of a company's outstanding shares of stock, calculated by the number of outstanding shares.

  • Market Maker:

    A brokerage firm or individual who facilitates trading by providing liquidity in the market, buying and selling securities to maintain a stable market.

  • Market Order:

    An order to buy or sell a security at the current market price.

  • Mutual Fund:

    An investment vehicle that pools money from multiple investors to invest in a diversified portfolio of stocks, bonds, or other securities.

  • Options:

    Financial derivatives that give the holder the right, but not the obligation, to buy or sell an underlying asset at a predetermined price on or before a specific expiration date.

  • Penny Stocks:

    Low-priced, speculative stocks of small companies with limited market capitalization and often traded over-the-counter (OTC).

  • Portfolio:

    A collection of financial assets, such as stocks, bonds, and cash equivalents, held by an individual, institution, or investment fund.

  • P/E Ratio:

    A valuation ratio that compares a company's current stock price to its per-share earnings. It helps investors assess the company's relative value and potential growth.

  • Return on Investment (ROI):

    A financial metric used to assess the profitability of an investment relative to its cost. It is calculated by dividing the net profit from the investment by the initial investment cost.

  • Securities:

    Financial instruments that represent ownership or debt in a publicly-traded company or a government entity.

  • Short Position:

    The sale of a borrowed financial instrument with the expectation that the price will decrease, allowing the investor to buy back the instrument at a lower price to return it to the lender and make a profit.

  • Short Selling:

    A trading strategy where an investor borrows shares of a security and sells them, anticipating that the price will decrease. The investor later buys back the shares at a lower price to return them to the lender, making a profit from the price difference.

  • Short Squeeze:

    A situation where a heavily shorted stock experiences a rapid price increase, forcing short sellers to buy back the stock to cover their positions, further driving the price higher.

  • Stock Market Index:

    A composite measurement that tracks the performance of a specific group of stocks, representing the overall market or a specific sector.

  • Stock Split:

    A corporate action where a company divides its existing shares into multiple shares, resulting in a decrease in the share price and an increase in the number of outstanding shares.

  • Stop Loss:

    A predefined price level set by an investor to limit potential losses on a trade if the market moves against them.

  • Technical Analysis:

    An investment analysis methodology that evaluates historical price and volume data to forecast future price movements and identify potential trading opportunities.

  • Ticker Symbol:

    An abbreviation used to uniquely identify a publicly traded company's stock for trading purposes. It is typically composed of letters.

  • Ticker Tape:

    A historical record of all the transactions and price changes in a particular market, displayed in a continuous scrolling format.

  • Trading Platform:

    The software or application used by investors and traders to execute trades and monitor financial markets.

  • Trading Volume:

    The total number of shares or contracts traded in a security or market during a given period of time.

  • Underlying Asset:

    The financial asset on which a derivative contract, such as options or futures, is based. The value of the derivative is derived from the value of the underlying asset.

  • Value Investing:

    An investment strategy that focuses on buying undervalued stocks or assets, anticipating their price will rise over time as the market recognizes their true value.

  • Volatility:

    The measure of how much the price of a financial instrument fluctuates over time. Higher volatility indicates larger price swings, while lower volatility suggests more stable prices.

  • Volatility Index (VIX):

    A popular measure of market volatility, often referred to as the "fear index," calculated based on S&P 500 index options.

  • Volume Weighted Average Price (VWAP):

    A trading benchmark that calculates the average price a security has traded at throughout the day, weighted by the volume of each trade.

  • Yield:

    The income earned from an investment, typically expressed as a percentage of the investment's cost or current value.

  • Zero-Coupon Bond:

    A type of bond that does not pay periodic interest, but is instead issued at a discount to its face value and matures at its face value.
